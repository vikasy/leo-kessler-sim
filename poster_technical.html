1  The five equations that decide everything

dΩ/dt = −(3/2) J₂ n (RE/p)² cos i
Position (plane orientation). Earth's bulge pivots every orbital plane; clouds smear from ring to shell in months — debris forgets where it came from.
da/dt = −ρ(h) · (CdA/m) · √(μa)
Altitude h and area-to-mass A/m. Density ρ falls exponentially with altitude: debris at 550 km clears in years, at 800+ km it lingers for decades. A/m is destiny — flakes fall fast, dense bodies linger.
N(>Lc) = 0.1 · M0.75 · Lc−1.71
Mass M. Fragment count grows as M0.75: our 270 kg event makes ~341 trackable + ~17,000 lethal 1–10 cm fragments. A 2,000 kg satellite would make ~4.5× more.
λ = Ni Nj σ vrel Δt / V,   σ = π(r₁+r₂)²
Size r and speed vrel. Collision rate = pairs × target area × distance swept ÷ volume. At 8–15 km/s closing speed, 1 kg carries ~25 kg-TNT of energy: >40 J/g shatters, it never dents.
R₀(N) = a N0.99 / (1 − N/N*)
Population N. R₀ = secondary collisions per breakup (an epidemic reproduction number). Near-linear in N until derelict feedback compounds it; diverges at the critical population N*.

2  Anatomy: which part of the satellite matters

Ranking for cascade risk: derelict drag area (dead-satellite tumbling A/2) > altitude > mass (fragment yield) ≈ hard-body size > anything else about the vehicle. Speed is fixed by orbital mechanics — every object is a hypervelocity projectile by default.

3  Parameter leverage (sensitivity, Table 6 of the paper)

Operations quality (avoidance, disposal, reliability) swings R₀ as much as any physical parameter — it is co-dominant with geometry. The derelict tumbling area is the leading model systematic and is reported with a factor-of-4 sweep.

4  The scaling law — and its breakdown

R₀ ∝ N⁰·⁹⁹ (near-linear, as kinetic theory predicts) until derelict production compounds it. The criticality crossing is bracketed by direct simulation — no extrapolation: R₀ = 0.31 ± 0.09 at 10⁵, 0.79 ± 0.22 at 1.5×10⁵, 1.92 at 2×10⁵.

5  The regime map — where is the boundary?

R₀ = 1 crossings bracketed directly per operations tier: ~2×10⁴ (poor) → ~4×10⁴ (degraded) → ~1.6×10⁵ (baseline) → >10⁶ (excellent). Roughly half a decade to a decade of shell population per operations tier: capacity and discipline are partially interchangeable currencies.

6  Validation & reproducibility

• J₂ nodal rate: −4.489°/day vs −4.49 analytic  • SBM fragments: 341 vs 341.7  • Derelict decay 1.1 yr (tumbling A/2) vs 1–5 yr observed  • Integrated LEGEND-like benchmark: catastrophic-collision rate 0.04–0.16/yr vs LEGEND's 0.11–0.20/yr; fragment growth 1.0–1.5×/century with no new launches  • dt-ladder convergence in the feedback regime  • Every number reruns in minutes from the archived code (Zenodo v1.0.1).

The conditional conclusion: a million satellites are not safe or unsafe in themselves. One 550-km shell holding all of them self-ignites; the same fleet across 40 low shells, operated at demonstrated avoidance/disposal standards, holds a ~35× margin — which collapses ~300× if operations degrade. Cascade stability is an engineered, conditional property: fly low, spread out, dispose reliably, keep dodging. A design requirement, not a reassurance.
