A MILLION SATELLITES?!

Can they all fit up there — and will they start crashing like dominoes?

1Is there room for a million?

Split 1,000,000 satellites across 40 lanes and each lane holds 25,000. Sounds crowded? Each satellite gets a private patch of sky bigger than the state of New Jersey, with its nearest neighbor about 150 km away.

YES — they fit, with room to spare.

2How do they stay in their lane?

The air up there is super thin, but it still slowly drags satellites down. So each one gives tiny engine puffs to climb back — like a bike rider making little wiggles to ride straight.

In our computer model, every satellite stayed within half a kilometer of its lane for 100 years. Onboard computers also dodge any junk coming their way.

3Uh-oh: what if junk hits one?

We ran the crash inside a computer (never in real life!): a 10 kg chunk of old rocket junk smashed into one satellite at about 30,000 km/h.

Result: about 340 big pieces and 17,000 tiny ones — every piece now a speeding bullet. Could this start a chain reaction?

4The domino question (Kessler Syndrome)

In 1978, scientist Don Kessler asked: could one crash make junk that causes more crashes, making more junk… like dominoes that never stop?

We measured each crash's "domino score": how many new crashes it causes. Score above 1 = snowball. Below 1 = the mess fades away.

5Good news: space cleans itself (down low)

That whisper-thin air acts like a slow vacuum cleaner: it tugs junk lower and lower until it burns up as shooting stars. In our model, half the junk was gone in about a year — and almost all of it within ten. (Way up high, junk can last centuries — so we keep constellations low!)

6What our 100-year experiment found

We simulated the constellation for 100 years, 30 times over, with real orbit physics: Earth's uneven gravity, air drag, and NASA's rules for how satellites shatter.

Zero chain reactions. After the crash, the junk cloud spread into a ring, then a shell around Earth — and then air drag swept it away faster than it could cause new crashes.

Then we tried 1,000,000 satellites crammed into a single lane — and the dominoes DID fall: the score shot over the danger line. But spread the same million across 40 lanes and the score drops to about 1-in-35 per lane — far below danger.

The rule that saves the day: SPREAD OUT. Dead satellites left drifting are the biggest troublemakers.

So… is a million satellites possible?

FEASIBLE? YES.  There is plenty of room, and lane-keeping tech already works today.

KESSLER RISK? REAL if crammed, small if spread out. One overcrowded lane snowballs; 40 lanes stay safe.

The 4 safety rules:

  1. Fly low — so junk burns up fast
  2. Spread out — many lanes, never one big crowd
  3. Bring dead satellites home — no drifting ghosts
  4. Keep dodging — computers steer around junk
Made with a physics simulation: 100-year mean-element orbital model (J2 gravity + air drag), station-keeping control, NASA Standard Breakup Model, kinetic collision theory, 30 Monte-Carlo runs. Numbers are careful estimates, not guarantees.
